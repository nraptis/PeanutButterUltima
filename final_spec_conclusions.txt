Final Specification Conclusions
==============================

Project conclusion
- The core engine direction is now stable: stream-based bundle/unbundle/recover, fixed archive format, strong block integrity checks, and practical recovery behavior for real-world disk damage.

Decision history (what we tried, what we learned)

Recovery header placement
- We tried recovery headers in L1 blocks.
- We moved recovery headers to L3 blocks.
- Final decision: L3 placement gave cleaner block-level recovery semantics and better alignment with streaming decode/recover behavior.

Archive discovery and ordering
- We tried archive-index header-flag based file sorting.
- We tried several by-file-name sorting flavors.
- Final decision: filename-template discovery with numeric index ordering and gap-aware archive boxes is the most practical and resilient for damaged/missing archive sets.

Checksum models
- We tried runs without checksums.
- We tried payload-only checksums.
- We tried payload + recovery-stride checksums.
- Final decision: strong checksum including payload + full recovery stride fields is the best model for corruption detection and recovery control.

Recovery stride model
- We tried more than 10 recovery stride variants.
- Final decision: validated stride semantics integrated with checksum verification produced the most reliable recovery behavior.

Record framing picks
- File content length field size (6-byte concept phase): final framing choice converged correctly on first decisive attempt.
- Path length field size (2-byte): chosen correctly on first attempt.
- Final structure remains straightforward and durable for streaming parse.

Metadata/layout variants
- We tried a files + manifest model with a separator flag.
- We tried a model where files and folders shared naming space.
- Final decision: direct record stream (path-length, path, content-length, content) with directory marker was simpler and more robust.

Fence/error experimentation
- We ran many fence/error-trial variants and simplifications.
- Final decision: reduce branchy flag complexity, keep trap-able error codes, and preserve recover-vs-strict behavioral separation.

Largest challenge
- The hardest problem was archive recovery under realistic corruption and missing-archive conditions.
- Final conclusion: the strong-checksum + recovery-stride model works excellently for archive recovery in the current design.

Future work
- Add dedicated recovery records (data sections packaged specifically for recovery) to make archives safer and improve recovery confidence under multi-region damage.

Philosophy manifesto
- Traditional archive tools were built for an era where saving bytes was the top priority.
- Real user pain now is often catastrophic failure modes: hard-drive failures, sector damage, and single-byte corruption causing full archive loss.
- Compression is no longer always the primary need.
- The stronger need is chunked archival systems that:
  a.) do not take forever to index, even with very large file sets,
  b.) fit practical filesystem constraints (including exFAT and FAT32 transfer workflows),
  c.) support strong layer-cake encryption,
  d.) provide strong recovery paths so total data loss is rare.

Final statement
- This project’s final direction prioritizes survivability, operational clarity, and recoverability over legacy assumptions.
